Who Has Won to Mastership

dog strength wild

Who has won to mastership — this was the question that hung over the team like a storm cloud waiting to break. Every dog knew it, sensed it in the tension between Buck and Spitz. The white dog’s reign was coming to an end; it was only a matter of when and how.

The trail to Dawson continued, mile after frozen mile. The dogs ran in silence, conserving their energy for the work ahead. But beneath the surface, the rivalry simmered and grew, fed by a thousand small incidents and slights.

Buck no longer waited for Spitz to lead. He began to assert himself, challenging the white dog’s authority in subtle ways. He would break trail when it wasn’t his turn. He would choose the camping spot. He would eat first, or at least try to.

“Buck stood and looked on, the successful champion, the dominant primordial beast who had made his kill and found it good.”

These provocations enraged Spitz, but the white dog was too cunning to attack directly. He waited for Buck to make a mistake, to expose a weakness that could be exploited. But Buck made no mistakes. The primordial beast within him was fully awake now, guiding his actions with ancient wisdom.

The other dogs sensed the shift in power and adjusted accordingly. Pike, once terrified of Spitz, began to disobey openly. Joe snarled back when Spitz corrected him. Even the usually placid Billee showed signs of rebellion. The pack was fracturing, and Buck was at the center of the divide.

François tried to maintain order, but he was fighting against nature itself. The hierarchy of the pack was not a human construct; it was something older and more fundamental. Buck’s rise to dominance was as natural as the turning of seasons, as unstoppable as the melting of spring ice.

The confrontation came on a clear night when the moon hung full and bright over the frozen landscape. A snowshoe rabbit broke from cover, and the dogs gave chase with wild abandon. Spitz, with his lifetime of experience, cut across to intercept. Buck, running on pure instinct, followed the rabbit’s trail directly.

They came together over the fallen rabbit — Buck’s kill, fairly won. But Spitz would not yield. The white dog attacked, and Buck met him head-on. The other dogs formed their ancient ring, watching with yellow eyes as the two champions fought.

The battle was brutal and decisive. Spitz was strong and experienced, but Buck was stronger, driven by something deeper than mere ambition. He fought with the fury of the primordial beast, with the wisdom of ancestral memory. And when it was over, Spitz lay defeated in the snow.
