Sons of the Wolf

wolf wild strength

Sons of the wolf — that’s what the old Tlingit called them, the big Northern dogs that hauled the sleds and carried the mail. They were not fully domesticated, these animals. The wolf blood ran strong in their veins, and it showed in their yellow eyes, their thick pelts, their savage independence.

The breeding of sled dogs was an art practiced by the indigenous peoples of the North for thousands of years. They selected for endurance, strength, and the ability to withstand brutal cold. The result was an animal perfectly adapted to its environment, capable of feats that no Southern dog could match.

But the gold rush brought new demands. The stampeders needed dogs — thousands of them — and they didn’t care about breeding or bloodlines. Any animal that could pull was pressed into service. Mastiffs, St. Bernards, collies, even small terriers were shipped north and harnessed to sleds.

Most of these imported dogs died quickly. They lacked the coat to survive the cold, the feet to handle the snow and ice, the temperament to endure the brutal work. The survivors interbred with the native dogs, creating new lines that combined Southern size with Northern hardiness.

“He was neither combatant nor concubine. He was, after all, a dog, and dogs were property, not persons, in the great scheme of things.”

The dogs themselves had no say in any of this. They lived and died at the whim of their masters, subject to the club and the fang, the harness and the trail. Yet there was a strange dignity in their endurance, a nobility that transcended their status as property.

Buck understood this better than most. He had been both pampered pet and brutal sled dog. He had experienced the best and worst that humans had to offer. In the end, he chose the wild, but not because he hated men. He chose it because the wolf blood in his veins demanded it, because the call was too strong to resist.

The pack he led now were his true family. They hunted together, slept together, howled at the moon together. When a new wolf joined the pack, Buck assessed it carefully, testing its strength and character. Only the worthy were accepted; the rest were driven away.

The hierarchy of the pack was strict but fair. Each wolf knew its place, and that place was determined by a complex mix of factors:

  • Strength and fighting ability
  • Intelligence and problem-solving skills
  • Hunting prowess
  • Loyalty to the pack
  • Age and experience

Buck stood at the top of this hierarchy, undisputed leader, the alpha among alphas. He had earned his position through countless challenges, countless demonstrations of his superiority. And he maintained it through constant vigilance, aware that younger wolves were always watching, always waiting for signs of weakness.

The winters were hardest, when prey was scarce and the cold was killing. But they were also the times when the pack was strongest, most unified. They moved as one organism, their coordination almost telepathic. A hunt that might take a single wolf days could be accomplished in hours by the working pack.

Spring brought new challenges — the birth of pups, the territorial disputes with neighboring packs, the abundance of prey that made everyone fat and lazy. Buck navigated these challenges with the wisdom of experience, keeping his pack safe through another cycle of seasons.

He was a son of the wolf, and he had come home at last. The blood of ancient hunters flowed in his veins, connecting him to an unbroken line stretching back to the dawn of time. He was exactly what he was meant to be.
