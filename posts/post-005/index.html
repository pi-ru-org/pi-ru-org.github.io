Into the Primitive

wild journey alaska

Old longings nomadic leap, chafing at custom’s chain; Again from its brumal sleep wakens the ferine strain. These words echoed through Buck’s consciousness as he journeyed deeper into the primitive world of the North. Each mile traveled was a mile further from civilization, a mile closer to something ancient and untamed.

The Dyea beach was a nightmare scene of chaos and confusion. Dogs howled, men cursed, and the endless procession of gold-seekers pushed northward toward the Chilkoot Pass. Buck observed it all with growing understanding, his keen intelligence cataloging every detail, learning the ways of this strange new world.

“He was beaten (he knew that); but he was not broken. He saw, once for all, that he stood no chance against a man with a club.”

The lesson had been hard but necessary. In the Southland, such brutality would have been unthinkable. Here, it was simply the way of things. Buck filed this knowledge away, adding it to his growing arsenal of survival skills. He would never forget, and he would never again make the mistake of challenging a man with a club in his hand.

François, the French-Canadian, proved to be a fair master. He knew dogs, understood their ways, and treated them with rough kindness. Perrault, the courier, cared only for the mail and the speed of their passage. Together, they made a competent team, driving the dogs hard but never beyond their limits.

The first night on the trail taught Buck another valuable lesson. There was no warm stable here, no bed of straw. The snow was his bed, and the stars his ceiling. He watched in bewilderment as the other dogs dug holes in the snow and curled up, disappearing beneath the white blanket.

That night, he nearly froze. But by the second night, he had learned. He dug his own hole, curled into a tight ball, and let the snow insulate him against the bitter cold. When he emerged in the morning, shaking off the white powder, he felt a strange satisfaction. He was adapting, becoming something new.

The trail to Dawson was long and treacherous. They crossed frozen rivers where the ice groaned ominously beneath their weight. They climbed mountain passes where the wind cut like a knife. They descended into valleys so deep that the sun was only a memory.

Through it all, Buck grew stronger. His muscles hardened, his endurance increased, and his mind sharpened. He learned to read the snow, to sense thin ice before it cracked, to find shelter in the most unlikely places. The primitive beast within him was awakening, stretching its ancient limbs.

The hierarchy of the team fascinated him. Spitz was the leader, a experienced dog who brooked no challenge to his authority. But Buck sensed weakness in the white dog, sensed opportunity. The confrontation was inevitable; it was only a matter of time.

For now, though, Buck was content to learn and grow. The trail had much to teach him, and he was an eager student. Every day brought new challenges, new opportunities to prove himself. And deep within, the call of the wild grew stronger, more insistent, impossible to ignore.
